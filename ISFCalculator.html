What Is Your Insulin Sensitivity Factor?

Your insulin sensitivity factor (ISF), also sometimes called a "correction factor" tells you how many points in milligrams per deciliter (mg/dL), your blood sugar will drop for each unit of insulin you take. Your ISF is used to help determine how much insulin you need daily.

There are two formulae used to calculate your ISF. The first applies if you use regular insulin such as Humulin or Novolin. The other applies if you use rapid-acting insulin such as insulin aspart (NovoLog) and insulin lispro (Humalog).



Regular Insulin

For regular insulin, use the "1800 rule". This tells you how much your blood sugar will drop for each unit of regular insulin you take.

For example, if your total daily dose of regular insulin (TDDregular) is 30 units, divide 1800 by 30. This equals 60.

This means your insulin sensitivity factor to regular insulin is 1:60, or that one unit of regular insulin will lower your blood sugar by about 60 mg/dL.



ISF=1800TDDregular


Rapid-acting Insulin

For rapid-acting insulin, use the "1500 rule". This tells you how much your blood sugar will drop for each unit of rapid-acting insulin you take.

For example, if your total daily dose of rapid-acting insulin (TDDrapid) is 30 units, divide 1500 by 30. This equals 50.

This means your insulin sensitivity factor to rapid-acting insulin is 1:50, or that one unit of rapid-acting insulin will lower your blood sugar by about 50 mg/dL.


ISF=1500TDDrapid



How Do You Determine Your Insulin Dosage?

Once you know how sensitive you are to insulin, you can figure out how much insulin you need to give yourself to lower your blood sugar by a certain amount.

For example, if your blood sugar is 200 mg/dL and you'd like to use your rapid-acting insulin to lower it to 125 mg/dL, you'd need your blood sugar to drop by 75 mg/dL.



drop=current-target


From the insulin sensitivity factor calculation, you know that your rapid-acting insulin sensitivity factor is 1:60. In other words, one unit of rapid-acting insulin lowers your blood sugar by about 60 mg/dL.

You'll need to divide the number of mg/dL you want to lower, which is 75, by your sensitivity factor, which is 60.



units=dropISF


Let's Put It All Together

We can roll all this math into two simple equations (one for regular, one for rapid-acting insulin) to quickly compute our ISF and how much insulin we need in order to bring down our blood sugar.



unitsregular=current-target1800*TDDregular

unitsrapid=current-target1500*TDDrapid
